About Turkmenistan

Learn about Turkmenistan’s history, traditions, and landscapes that define its identity

Geography

Turkmenistan occupies a strategic location with diverse landscapes spanning vast territories. The country covers an area of 491,210 square kilometers, divided into distinct regions each with unique characteristics. It emerged as an independent state in 1991.

The national capital is Ashgabat. There are five administrative subdivisions, called velayat, or region, each with its own regional capital:

  • Ahal Velayat (Anau)
  • Balkan Velayat (Balkanabad)
  • Mary Velayat (Mary)
  • Dashoguz Velayat (Dashoguz)
  • Lebap Velayat (Turkmenabad)

History

Turkmenistan has a deep and complex history that dates back thousands of years, with evidence of early civilizations such as the Margiana culture thriving in the region. It became a key part of the Silk Road, allowing cities like Merv to grow into major centers of trade, culture, and Islamic scholarship during the medieval period.

Throughout its history, the region was ruled by powerful empires, including the Achaemenid Persian Empire, the forces of Alexander the Great, and later the Seljuk Turks and Mongol Empire. These influences shaped the country’s culture, architecture, and traditions, many of which are still visible today. In the 19th century, Turkmenistan was gradually conquered by the Russian Empire, and it later became the Turkmen Soviet Socialist Republic under the Soviet Union. After the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991, Turkmenistan declared independence and has since developed as a sovereign nation with its own political system and cultural identity.

Cuisine

Turkmen cuisine is varied, and includes both meat dishes and soups, and as well as plenty of fruits and vegetables. Bread is a must for every meal. A piece of fresh Turkmen flat round bread, churek, baked at home in clay ovens, is delicious in combination with any meal or snack.

Plov (pilaf) is considered the king of dishes, prepared for celebrations and gatherings. Each region has its own variation, reflecting local ingredients and preferences. Most cafes and restaurants cook up a combination of Turkmen and Russian dishes, and offer a large list of salads and side dishes (rice, fried potatoes, pasta, and mashed potatoes). None of the dishes are heavy on spices, but herbs such as parsley, coriander, basil and dill are frequently added.

Turkmens Famous Delicacies

Plov

The staple dish of Turkmenistan, it is a rich mixture of rice, lamb, carrots, onions, and oil, often served at celebrations.

Dograma

Hearty dish prepared for large gatherings, made by tearing thin bread into pieces and mixing it with boiled meat and onions, served with broth.

Tamdyrlama

A highly regarded specialty consisting of lamb cooked slowly inside a tamdyr (clay oven), resulting in tender meat without direct flame contact.

Gutap

Crescent-shaped pan-fried pastries stuffed with various fillings, most commonly spiced meat, onions, herbs, or spinach.

Chorek

A sacred, thick round bread baked in a tamdyr (clay oven), essential to every meal.

Arts & Crafts

For Turkmen people carpets are not only a symbol of beauty and happiness, but also tell the story of their forefathers; carpets tell Turkmen history. For centuries, travelers through Turkmenistan document about the quality of the carpets woven here.

Although carpets were already being produced in the time of the Seljuks, the first written reference to Turkmen carpets was made by Marco Polo (XIII c). In the XIV c Ibn Batutta also writes about woolen carpets in homes of inhabitants of (Kunya) Urgench, and of silk carpets in the palace of Kutlug-Timur there. We can also find Turkmen carpets back in the paintings of the masters of the Italian Renaissance (XIV-XV cc), and (probably) in the miniatures of Timurid times such as the Shah-name (XV c).

Festivals

Turkmenistan’s festivals and celebrations are an important reflection of the country’s rich cultural heritage and traditions. One of the most significant celebrations is Nowruz, which marks the arrival of spring and is celebrated with traditional food, music, and community gatherings.

During national holidays, people enjoy folk dances, performances, and wear colorful traditional clothing. Events often include horse racing, especially featuring the famous Akhal-Teke horses, which are a symbol of national pride. Independence Day and Neutrality Day are also widely celebrated with parades, fireworks, and cultural shows. These festivals help preserve Turkmen identity while bringing communities together in a spirit of unity and joy.

Nature

A boring landscape?

In fact, Turkmenistan holds some of the most fascinating diversity of landscapes — from moving sand dunes and limestone cliffs to lush river valleys and mountains formed in the Jurassic period. There are also volcanoes, waterfalls, lakes, and of course, the Caspian Sea.

Moreover, the country hosts several nature reserves that protect unique and endangered species of flora and fauna. Below, you will find descriptions of some of the best-known nature reserves, their protected species, and other notable features.All nature reserves in Turkmenistan are protected areas and not open to the public, except for scientists with approval from the Ministry of Nature Protection.

Ahalteke Horses

The Akhalteke horse, known as the “golden horse of Turkmenistan,” is one of the oldest and most elegant horse breeds in the world. Its shimmering coat and unmatched endurance have made it a symbol of pride and strength for our nation.

Experts say that nowhere in the world but in Turkmenistan nature and man’s efforts have succeeded in creating such a graceful, exceptional, tireless, sturdy, noble horse as the Akhal Teke. They are not only fast and of great endurance but also of peculiar royal beauty. These horses became one of the most striking illustrations of talent and mastery of Turkmen people. Turkmen are very proud of their horses.

Today, they remain a national treasure and are celebrated annually in festivals and races that honor their beauty, grace, and the deep bond between the Turkmen people and their horses
